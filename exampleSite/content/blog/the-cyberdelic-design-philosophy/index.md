## Where Two Worlds Collide

The cyberdelic aesthetic is a marriage of two seemingly disparate visual traditions. On one side,
the swirling, kaleidoscopic energy of 1960s psychedelic concert posters — hand-drawn letterforms,
vibrating colors, organic shapes that resist the grid. On the other, the cold precision of
cyberpunk: holographic displays, neon-lit rain, the glow of a cathode ray tube in a dark room.

> The future is already here — it's just not evenly distributed. — William Gibson

## Design Principles

### 1. Dark Backgrounds, Bright Accents

Cyberdelic design relies on the contrast between deep, dark backgrounds and intensely bright accent
colors. The background is not merely "dark mode" — it's a void, an infinite space from which light
emerges.

### 2. Neon Glow as a Design Element

Glow effects are not decorative flourishes; they are structural. In cyberdelic design, a text shadow
or box shadow with a neon color creates depth, hierarchy, and emotional resonance.

### 3. The Glitch as Texture

Digital artifacts — scan lines, chromatic aberration, data corruption — are treated as visual
texture rather than errors. The glitch is a window into the substrate of the digital world.

```css
.glitch-text {
  text-shadow:
    0 0 20px rgba(56, 71, 255, 0.5),
    0 0 40px rgba(255, 0, 170, 0.5);
  animation: glitch-skew 4s infinite;
}
```

## Color Palette

The cyberdelic palette centers on a violet-black void with four primary accent colors:

| Token   | Hex       | Usage                  |
| ------- | --------- | ---------------------- |
| Blue    | `#3847FF` | Links, primary actions |
| Magenta | `#FF00AA` | Emphasis, selection    |
| Cyan    | `#00FFFF` | Borders, highlights    |
| Amber   | `#FFB000` | Dates, metadata        |

## Conclusion

Cyberdelic is not a trend — it's a visual language for the experience of living in an era where the
digital and physical are inseparable. The glowing screen is our campfire, the glitch is our folk
art, and the void is our canvas.
